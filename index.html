States of Matter — The Particle Model

Topic 1 · Solids, liquids, gases & particle behaviour

The three states

All matter is made up of tiny particles. The way these particles are arranged and how they move determines whether a substance is a solid, liquid or gas.

StateArrangementMovementForces
SolidClosely packed, regularVibrate in fixed positionsStrong
LiquidClosely packed, irregularMove past each otherModerate
GasFar apart, randomMove quickly in all directionsVery weak / negligible

Gas pressure

The pressure of a gas is caused by gas particles colliding with the walls of their container. The more frequent or harder the collisions, the higher the pressure. Heating a gas makes the particles move faster, so they collide harder and more often — pressure increases.

Diffusion

Diffusion is the spreading out of particles from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration. It happens because particles are in constant random motion. Examples: smelling perfume across a room (gas diffusion), purple potassium manganate(VII) spreading through water (solid in solution).

Diffusion is fastest in gases (particles move most quickly) and slowest in liquids. It does not happen in solids because particles cannot move past each other.

Changes of state

When a substance changes state, the particles' arrangement and motion change — but the particles themselves do not change. Energy is needed to overcome the forces between particles.

  • Melting: solid → liquid
  • Boiling / evaporating: liquid → gas
  • Condensing: gas → liquid
  • Freezing: liquid → solid
  • Sublimation: solid → gas directly (e.g. iodine, dry ice / solid CO₂)
Practicals: melting solids (e.g. sulfur, ice); boiling liquids (e.g. water); subliming solids (e.g. iodine); diffusion of gases (e.g. ammonia); diffusion of solids in solutions (e.g. potassium manganate(VII), KMnO₄).

Atoms and Elements

Topic 2 · The building blocks of matter

The Dalton atomic model

John Dalton proposed that all matter is made of tiny indivisible particles called atoms. The simple Dalton model states:

  • All matter is made of atoms.
  • Atoms cannot be created or destroyed (in chemical reactions).
  • Atoms of the same element are identical.
  • Atoms of different elements differ.

Elements

An element is a substance made of only one type of atom. There are about 100 elements in the Periodic Table. Each element has a chemical symbol — one or two letters. The first letter is always a capital; the second (if any) is always lowercase. A symbol always represents one atom of that element.

Compounds and Molecules

Topic 3 · Combining atoms

Compounds

A compound is a substance made of two or more different elements chemically joined together. Although there are only about 100 elements, they can combine in many different ways to form millions of different compounds.

Molecules and formulae

A molecule is a particle made of two or more atoms chemically joined. A formula shows the elements present and the ratio of atoms. The small number (subscript) tells you how many atoms of the element before it are in the molecule.

For example, H₂O contains 2 atoms of hydrogen and 1 atom of oxygen.

Properties of compounds

A compound has different properties from the elements it is made from. For example:

  • Iron (a magnetic, grey metal) + sulfur (a yellow non-metal powder) → iron sulfide (a non-magnetic, dark grey solid that does not behave like either element).
  • Sodium (a soft, reactive metal) + chlorine (a poisonous green gas) → sodium chloride (table salt — safe to eat).
Practical: Make iron sulfide from its elements by heating iron filings and sulfur powder. Compare the properties of Fe, S and FeS — the product is a new substance with different properties from either element.

Pure Substances

Topic 4 · Purity and the special properties of water

What is a pure substance?

A pure substance is made of only one type of particle — either one element or one compound. Mixtures are not pure.

Identifying pure substances by melting and boiling points

A pure substance has a fixed melting point and boiling point. An impure substance (a mixture) melts and boils over a range of temperatures.

  • Pure water boils at exactly 100 °C and freezes at exactly 0 °C (at atmospheric pressure).
  • Salty water boils above 100 °C and freezes below 0 °C — and both happen over a range.

Evaporation vs boiling

Both turn liquid into gas, but:

  • Boiling happens at the boiling point throughout the liquid.
  • Evaporation can happen at any temperature, only at the surface of the liquid.

The anomalous properties of water

Water behaves unusually compared with other small molecules:

  • Ice is less dense than water — so ice floats. Most substances are denser as solids than as liquids. This is why pipes can burst in winter and rocks crack as water freezes inside them.
  • Water has a high boiling point for such a small molecule.
  • Water is the only common substance that exists naturally as a solid, liquid and gas at typical Earth temperatures.
Practicals: measure the melting point of a pure solid (e.g. stearic acid) and an impure substance (e.g. candle wax); observe ice floating on water; freeze a plastic bottle full of water (it bulges/cracks).

Mixtures including Solutions

Topic 5 · Mixtures, solutions and air

What is a mixture?

A mixture contains two or more substances that are not chemically joined. The substances keep their own properties and can usually be separated by a physical process.

Solutions and saturation

A solution is a special type of mixture: a solute (e.g. salt) dissolved in a solvent (e.g. water). A saturated solution is one that has dissolved as much solute as it possibly can at that temperature — any more solute added will not dissolve.

Heating a solvent usually allows it to dissolve more solute (so a hot saturated solution becomes unsaturated when more solvent capacity opens up — but cooling it back down will cause some solute to crystallise out).

Air — a mixture of gases

Air is a mixture, not a compound. Approximate composition by volume:

  • Nitrogen — about 78%
  • Oxygen — about 21%
  • Argon — about 0.9%
  • Carbon dioxide — about 0.04%
  • Plus water vapour and other trace gases

Uses of oxygen

Oxygen is essential for respiration (in living things) and for combustion (burning). Oxygen and carbon dioxide are exchanged between living things and the atmosphere through respiration and photosynthesis.

Practicals: Show that an Fe/S mixture has the same properties as the elements (each iron filing is still magnetic). Determine the proportion of oxygen in air by burning a candle in a sealed jar (water rises to fill ~⅕ of the volume) or using rusting iron wool over time.

Separating Mixtures

Topic 6 · Distillation, chromatography & choosing a method

Distillation

Distillation separates a liquid from a mixture by boiling it off and then condensing the vapour back into a pure liquid. It works because different substances have different boiling points.

  • Heat the mixture until the substance with the lowest boiling point evaporates.
  • The vapour rises and passes through a condenser (cooled by water).
  • It condenses back to a pure liquid called the distillate.

Example: obtaining pure water from sea water — water boils at 100 °C and is collected as distillate; the salt is left behind. Evaporating sea water leaves salt; evaporating tap water leaves a small residue (dissolved minerals); evaporating pure water leaves nothing.

Chromatography

Chromatography separates substances dissolved in a solvent — useful for identifying coloured substances such as inks, dyes and food colourings.

  • Spot the mixture near the bottom of a strip of chromatography paper.
  • Stand the strip in a small amount of solvent (water or propanone). The solvent must be below the spots.
  • The solvent moves up the paper, carrying the substances with it. Different substances travel different distances depending on how much they dissolve in the solvent and how strongly they stick to the paper.
  • The separated spots can be compared with known reference substances.

Other separation methods (recap from 11+)

  • Filtration — separates an insoluble solid from a liquid (the residue is left in the filter; the filtrate passes through).
  • Evaporation — recovers a dissolved solid from a solution.
  • Decanting — pouring a liquid off a settled solid.
Practicals: distillation of ink or sea water to recover pure water; effect of evaporating tap water, pure water and sea water; chromatography of felt-tip pen inks or M&Ms / Smarties using water or propanone as the solvent.

Combustion — Chemical Reactions

Topic 7 · Burning, gas tests and conservation of mass

Using a Bunsen burner

The Bunsen has two flames:

  • Yellow (safety) flame — air hole closed; cool, sooty, easy to see.
  • Blue (roaring) flame — air hole open; hot, clean, used for heating.

Combustion of elements

Burning is a chemical reaction with oxygen, producing an oxide and releasing energy as heat and light.

  • magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide (bright white flame; white powder)
  • sulfur + oxygen → sulfur dioxide (blue flame; choking gas)
  • carbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide

Conservation of mass

In a chemical reaction, atoms are rearranged — not created or destroyed. So the total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants. When magnesium burns, the magnesium oxide produced weighs more than the magnesium started with — because oxygen has been added from the air.

Tests for gases

GasTestPositive result
OxygenInsert a glowing splintSplint relights
Carbon dioxideBubble through limewaterLimewater turns milky/cloudy
HydrogenInsert a lighted splintBurns with a squeaky pop

Test for water

Add the liquid to anhydrous (white) copper sulfate. If water is present, the powder turns from white to blue.

Word equations

Word equations show what reacts and what is produced. The reactants go on the left, the products on the right, separated by an arrow. State symbols can be added: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous solution.

Practicals: burn elements in air and oxygen; investigate mass changes when burning Mg (mass increases as oxygen is added).

Fuels and Production of Carbon Dioxide

Topic 8 · Combustion of fuels and pollution

Fuels and combustion

A fuel is a substance that releases energy when burned. Hydrocarbons (compounds of hydrogen and carbon only — e.g. methane CH₄, petrol) and coal are common fuels. Fossil fuels (coal, crude oil, natural gas) were formed from the remains of dead plants and animals over millions of years.

Complete combustion

When a hydrocarbon burns in plenty of oxygen:

hydrocarbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water

For methane: methane + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water

Incomplete combustion

If there is not enough oxygen, hydrocarbons burn incompletely, producing carbon monoxide (CO) and even soot (carbon, C). Carbon monoxide is colourless, odourless and very toxic — it stops the blood from carrying oxygen.

Polluting gases and the environment

  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — a greenhouse gas. Increasing levels from burning fossil fuels are causing climate change / global warming.
  • Carbon monoxide (CO) — toxic; from incomplete combustion (e.g. faulty boilers, car engines).
  • Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) — produced when fuels containing sulfur (especially coal) burn. Dissolves in rain water to form acid rain, which damages buildings (especially limestone), kills aquatic life and harms forests.
  • Nitrogen oxides (NOx) — also contribute to acid rain.
Practical: Test the products of burning natural gas or a candle. Hold a cold dry beaker above a flame — water condenses (test with anhydrous copper sulfate). Hold a beaker of limewater above the flame — it turns milky, showing CO₂ is produced.

Oxidation of Metals

Topic 9 · Reactions of metals and rusting

Reactions of metals with oxygen

Metals burn or tarnish in oxygen to form metal oxides:

metal + oxygen → metal oxide

e.g. magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide (brilliant white flame)

Reactions of metals with water

Reactive metals react with water to give a metal hydroxide and hydrogen:

metal + water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen

e.g. sodium fizzes vigorously on cold water. Magnesium reacts only very slowly with cold water but quickly with steam:

magnesium + steam → magnesium oxide + hydrogen

Reactions of metals with acids

metal + acid → salt + hydrogen

e.g. magnesium + hydrochloric acid → magnesium chloride + hydrogen

The reaction is observed by fizzing (bubbles of hydrogen).

Test for hydrogen

Insert a lighted splint into the gas — it burns with a squeaky pop.

Rusting of iron

Iron rusts to form hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust). Rusting requires both air (oxygen) and water. Salt speeds it up.

Methods of preventing rusting:

  • Painting — barrier between iron and air/water.
  • Oiling / greasing — same idea, used for moving parts.
  • Galvanising — coating with zinc; even if scratched, the more reactive zinc corrodes preferentially (sacrificial protection).
  • Coating with plastic — e.g. bicycle wheel rims.
  • Alloying — making stainless steel (iron + chromium + nickel).
Practicals: react Mg with air, cold water, steam and acid (compare reactivity). Investigate rusting using three test tubes: iron + air only (no rust); iron + boiled water + oil layer to exclude air (no rust); iron + air + water (rusts) — proving both air and water are needed.

Thermal Decomposition Reactions

Topic 10 · Breaking down compounds with heat

What is thermal decomposition?

Thermal decomposition is when a compound breaks down into simpler substances on heating. It is a chemical reaction (not reversible by simple cooling).

Heating copper carbonate

copper carbonate → copper oxide + carbon dioxide

Observations: the green powder (CuCO₃) turns black (CuO). The CO₂ produced can be tested with limewater (turns milky).

Heating calcium carbonate

calcium carbonate → calcium oxide + carbon dioxide

This is the reaction used to make quicklime (calcium oxide) from limestone in industry.

Heating hydrated copper(II) sulfate

hydrated copper sulfate → anhydrous copper sulfate + water

Observations: the blue hydrated crystals turn white as water is driven off. Adding water to the white powder turns it back to blue — this is also the test for water.

Substances that don't decompose on heating

Some substances are stable to heat. For example, copper oxide (CuO) does not change chemically when heated — it just gets hot.

Practicals: heat copper carbonate in a test tube, observing the colour change and testing the gas produced with limewater. Heat hydrated copper sulfate (blue → white), then add water to the cool product (white → blue).

Acids and Alkalis

Topic 11 · The pH scale and indicators

The pH scale

The pH scale runs from 0 to 14 and shows how acidic or alkaline a solution is.

  • pH 0–6 — acidic (lower number = stronger acid)
  • pH 7 — neutral (e.g. pure water)
  • pH 8–14 — alkaline (higher number = stronger alkali)

Indicators

IndicatorIn acidNeutralIn alkali
LitmusRedPurpleBlue
Universal IndicatorRed/orange/yellowGreenBlue/purple
PhenolphthaleinColourlessColourlessPink

A pH meter gives a more accurate numerical reading than indicators.

Common acids and alkalis

  • Acids: hydrochloric (HCl), sulfuric (H₂SO₄), nitric (HNO₃), citric, ethanoic (vinegar).
  • Alkalis: sodium hydroxide (NaOH), calcium hydroxide / limewater, ammonia.

Acid/base properties of oxides

  • Metal oxides are basic — they react with acids to form salts and water (e.g. magnesium oxide, copper oxide).
  • Non-metal oxides are acidic — they dissolve in water to form acidic solutions (e.g. CO₂ in water gives carbonic acid; SO₂ gives sulfurous acid — links to acid rain).

Neutralisation

An acid plus an alkali (or base) produces a neutral salt and water:

acid + alkali → salt + water

The pH moves towards 7 as more alkali is added to acid (or vice versa).

Practicals: testing acids and alkalis with litmus and Universal Indicator; measuring pH using Universal Indicator and a pH meter; adding alkali drop-by-drop to acid and tracking the change in pH.

Neutralisation Reactions

Topic 12 · Reactions of acids with bases, metals and carbonates

The four key reactions of acids

Acids react with metals, alkalis, metal oxides and metal carbonates. All these reactions form salts.

  1. Acid + metal → salt + hydrogen
    magnesium + sulfuric acid → magnesium sulfate + hydrogen (fizzing — H₂ gas)
  2. Acid + alkali → salt + water (neutralisation)
    hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide → sodium chloride + water
  3. Acid + metal oxide → salt + water (the metal oxide is a base)
    sulfuric acid + copper oxide → copper sulfate + water
  4. Acid + metal carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide (fizzing — CO₂ gas)
    hydrochloric acid + calcium carbonate → calcium chloride + water + carbon dioxide

Naming salts

The salt's first name comes from the metal; the second name comes from the acid:

  • Hydrochloric acid → chloride salts
  • Sulfuric acid → sulfate salts
  • Nitric acid → nitrate salts

Acidity in the environment

Acid rain is rain with a pH below about 5.6, caused by sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides from burning fossil fuels dissolving in atmospheric water. Effects:

  • Damages stone buildings and statues — especially those made of limestone (calcium carbonate) or marble, which react with the acid.
  • Acidifies lakes and rivers, killing fish and other aquatic life.
  • Damages trees and leaches nutrients from soil.

Uses of limestone

  • Building material — but vulnerable to acid rain.
  • Agricultural lime — spread on fields to neutralise acidic soils, helping crops grow.
  • Cement and concrete production.
Practical: reaction of an acid (e.g. HCl) with an alkali, a metal, a metal oxide, and a metal carbonate. Test the gases produced (limewater for CO₂; squeaky pop for H₂).
